# Ordinary People: The Mass Psychology of Iran

This document profiles the ordinary Iranians whose collective behavior determines political outcomes. The regime survives or falls based on whether these people rise up, comply, or actively support. Understanding them is essential.

---

## WHY THE MASSES MATTER

Political science insight: Revolutions happen when ordinary people conclude:
1. The regime has lost legitimacy
2. The regime can be beaten
3. Enough others will join that individual risk is worth taking
4. What comes after will be better than what exists

All four conditions must be met. If any one fails, people stay home.

The characters in this document represent the millions who will decide Iran's future - not through ideology, but through countless individual calculations about risk, reward, and hope.

- Economic demands could unify otherwise fractured opposition

**What he believes:**

- The revolution promised to help the poor; it lied
- But the opposition is also all about cities
- At least the regime gives some handouts
- Politics is for others; survival is for him

**What he fears:**
- Complete loss of water (existential)
- Being forced off land (cultural death)
- Violence/chaos spreading to rural areas
- His children never coming back
- Dying alone, village empty

**His calculation:**
- Regime gives occasional assistance (however meager)
- Opposition promises nothing specific for him
- Change might be worse; who knows

- Water/agriculture policy that matters

**Strategic implications:**
- Rural poor are often regime supporters (or at least not opponents)
- This is partly about material dependence, partly about being ignored

- They're the least engaged in opposition politics

## THE SILENT MAJORITY: MODELING

### Why People Don't Act

```python
def will_participate(person, protest_event):
    # Calculate expected costs
    physical_risk = estimate_physical_risk(protest_event)
    economic_risk = estimate_economic_risk(person)
    family_risk = person.family_exposure * protest_event.visibility

    total_cost = physical_risk + economic_risk + family_risk

    # Calculate expected benefits
    individual_benefit = 0  # Marginal; one person doesn't change outcome
    emotional_benefit = person.grievance_level * 0.5
    social_benefit = peer_participation_rate * 10

    # The collective action problem:
    # Individual benefit is near zero
    # Individual cost is high
    # Rational to free-ride

    # What overcomes this:
    # 1. Emotional benefit (can't stand it anymore)
    # 2. Social benefit (friends are going, identity is at stake)
    # 3. Belief regime will fall (being on winning side)

    expected_benefit = emotional_benefit + social_benefit

    # Key factor: belief in success
    success_probability = person.belief_regime_survival
    if success_probability < 0.3:  # Regime seems weak
        expected_benefit *= 3  # Worth the risk

    return expected_benefit > total_cost
```

### The Cascade Model

```yaml
PROTEST_CASCADE:
  stage_1_activists:
    # People who protest regardless of others
    population_share: 3-5%

    # People who join when it seems like "everyone" is
    population_share: 30-40%
    threshold: 0.15-0.25  # Need to see 15-25% participating
    characteristics: [grievances_but_cautious, follow_peers, risk_averse]

  stage_4_late_joiners:
    # People who join when regime seems to be losing
    population_share: 20-30%
    threshold: 0.40+  # Need to see clear momentum
    characteristics: [very_risk_averse, material_interests, opportunists]

  never_join:
    # Regime supporters, too scared, too apathetic
    population_share: 20-30%
    characteristics: [regime_beneficiaries, extremely_risk_averse, apolitical]

  cascade_dynamics:
    - If stage_1 is crushed before stage_2 joins → movement dies
    - If stage_2 joins and builds → stage_3 threshold approached
    - If stage_3 joins → regime faces existential threat
    - Speed matters: slow build allows regime response; fast cascade overwhelms
```

### Economic vs. Political Mobilization

```yaml
ECONOMIC_MOBILIZATION:
  trigger: Direct material harm (wages unpaid, prices spike, job loss)
  participants: Workers, bazaaris, affected sectors
  demands: Usually specific (pay us, lower prices, give jobs)
  trajectory: Can remain narrow or can generalize to political

POLITICAL_MOBILIZATION:
  trigger: Political event (death in custody, election fraud, crackdown)
  participants: Students, activists, grievance-driven
  demands: Usually systemic (end regime, freedom, accountability)
  trajectory: Harder to sustain without economic base

REGIME VULNERABILITY:
  - Pure political protest → can be crushed
  - Pure economic protest → can be bought off
  - Economic + political fusion → most dangerous for regime
  - 2022 was political trigger → didn't fully fuse with economic
  - Future: economic crisis could create fusion
```

## CONCLUSION

Iran's political future will be determined by millions of individual decisions:
- Whether Mina joins the protest or stays home

- Whether Ahmad in the village supports or ignores change

**No elite strategy succeeds without mass participation.**
**Mass participation requires solving the collective action problem.**
**Solving it requires making participation seem:**
1. Likely to succeed
2. Shared by many others
3. Worth the individual risk
4. Connected to a better future

The game models these dynamics because they determine outcomes. A player who only thinks about elites will lose. A player who understands the masses will win.
