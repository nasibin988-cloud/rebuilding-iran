# IRAN 14XX: Core Vision

## The Elevator Pitch

A comprehensive political simulation where you shape Iran's future - from inside the regime or in opposition, as a nobody or a king. Every choice has consequences. There are no right answers, only tradeoffs. The measure of success isn't democracy - it's whether the Iranian people flourish.

---

## Why This Game Exists

### The Educational Gap

Iranians (and the world) need to understand:
- Why Iran is stuck in its current equilibrium
- How transitions actually work (and fail)
- What tradeoffs any path forward involves
- Why their opponents think and act as they do
- That governing is hard, and criticism is easy

Books teach. Lectures inform. But **games create understanding** through experience.

### The Empathy Gap

It's easy to hate the Sepahi, the Basiji, the torturer. But:
- How did they become what they are?
- What choices did they face?
- What do they fear losing?
- Could you, in their circumstances, have done differently?

This game forces you to understand - not excuse, but understand - every actor. Including the villains. **Especially** the villains.

### The Complexity Gap

Political discourse is dominated by:
- "If only we did X, everything would be fine"
- "The problem is just [one group/person]"
- "There's an obvious solution they refuse to implement"

Reality is: every solution creates new problems. Every victory has costs. Every coalition contains contradictions. This game makes that visceral.

## Core Philosophy

### 1. Eudaimonia, Not Democracy

The goal is human flourishing - people living good lives with dignity, prosperity, security, freedom, and meaning.

Democracy is often the best path to flourishing. But:
- A chaotic democracy that produces misery is a failure
- A benevolent authoritarianism that produces flourishing is... complicated
- The game doesn't tell you what to think. It shows you outcomes.

**The Flourishing Index measures:**
- Material wellbeing (can people eat? afford housing? find work?)
- Health (do people live long, healthy lives?)
- Freedom (can people speak, worship, associate, love?)
- Security (are people safe from violence, crime, arbitrary power?)
- Social cohesion (do people trust each other? is society fractured?)
- Cultural flourishing (do arts, education, identity thrive?)
- Sustainability (will this last? or is it mortgaging the future?)

A military dictator who scores 75% is "better" than a democrat who scores 40%. **This will make players uncomfortable. Good.**

### 2. No Villains, Only Humans

Every character - from Khamenei to a random Basiji - has:
- **A logic**: reasons that make sense from their perspective
- **Fears**: something they're trying to protect or avoid
- **Constraints**: limits on what they can actually do
- **Information**: what they know and don't know
- **History**: how they became who they are

The Sepah commander who orders violence might be:
- A true believer saving Iran from Western corruption
- A trapped man who did one terrible thing and can't go back
- A cynical careerist who sees no other path
- A father protecting his family from the consequences of defection
- All of the above, shifting depending on the day

**Players should finish the game able to explain, in good faith, why their opponents did what they did.**

### 3. Game Theory Made Visceral

Iran's current situation is a Nash equilibrium - stable not because anyone likes it, but because no one can unilaterally improve their position.

The game models:
- **Incentives**: What does each actor actually want?
- **Information**: What do they know about others' intentions?
- **Commitment problems**: Why can't enemies trust each other's promises?
- **Coordination failures**: Why can't natural allies work together?
- **Collective action problems**: Why don't the masses simply rise up?
- **Signaling**: What do actions reveal about intentions?
- **Focal points**: How do people coordinate without communication?

When players understand these dynamics, they understand why change is hard - and what might actually work.

### 4. Consequences Are Everything

Every choice echoes forward. Nothing is forgotten.

- Spare an enemy today → they might reconcile, or might return with vengeance
- Seize emergency powers "temporarily" → the temptation to keep them compounds
- Promise the Kurds autonomy → your nationalist allies remember
- Compromise with the clergy → your secular base feels betrayed
- Purge the old regime → their children grow up with grievances

The game tracks everything. Consequences arrive delayed, transformed, unexpected. Players learn that **the present is the past's consequence, and the future is being written now.**

### 5. Radical Perspectivism

Playing as Khamenei is a legitimate, educational experience.

From his perspective:
- He's holding together a country against Western imperialism
- The revolution's ideals are sacred and worth protecting
- Compromise is betrayal of the martyrs
- The alternative to his rule is chaos (and he might be right)

Playing as him teaches you why reform from within is so hard, why authoritarian leaders make the choices they do, why systems perpetuate themselves.

**You cannot defeat what you do not understand.**

### 6. History as Teacher

The game is grounded in real political science and historical cases.

Every mechanic links to:
- Scholarly literature (those 800 books)
- Historical parallels (Spain, Poland, South Africa, etc.)
- Case studies (what worked, what failed, why)

After a decision, players can ask: "Has this happened before?" And the game shows them.

### 7. Beauty and Craft

This is not a spreadsheet. Not a wall of text. Not "educational" in the boring sense.

It is:
- Visually beautiful (Persian aesthetics, painterly portraits, elegant UI)
- Emotionally engaging (characters you care about, stakes that matter)
- Narratively compelling (stories you want to see through)
- Replayable (different characters = different games)

**If it's not fun, no one will learn anything.**

## What This Game Is NOT

### Not Propaganda

The game doesn't tell you:
- Democracy is best
- Monarchy is best
- Secularism is best
- Islam is the problem
- The West is the savior

It shows you: these are the options, these are the tradeoffs, these are the consequences. **You decide.**

### Not Wish Fulfillment

There's no path where:
- Everyone gets what they want
- The bad guys are simply defeated
- Change comes without cost
- The right answer is obvious

Every ending involves loss. Every victory is partial. This is realism.

### Not Simplified

We don't pretend:
- Iran is just Tehran
- All Persians are the same
- Ethnicity is simple
- Religion is monolithic
- The opposition is unified
- International actors are consistent

The full complexity is modeled. Players who want easy answers will be frustrated. Good.

### Not Neutral (In One Sense)

The game IS committed to:
- Human dignity is real and matters
- Truth is better than lies
- Understanding is better than ignorance
- Flourishing is better than suffering

It is NOT committed to:
- Any particular political system
- Any particular faction's victory
- Any particular ideology's triumph

## The Player's Journey

### First Playthrough

Player chooses a familiar role (opposition figure, diaspora).
- Experiences hope, setbacks, difficult choices
- Probably achieves partial success
- Learns that governing is harder than criticizing

### Second Playthrough

Player tries different faction, different approach.
- Sees how earlier choices created later constraints
- Realizes opponents had reasons
- Starts to see the system, not just the actors

### Third+ Playthroughs

Player experiments with "what if."
- What if Khamenei had reformed early?
- What if the opposition had stayed unified?
- What if the Kurds had been accommodated?
- What if the West had stayed out?

Each playthrough teaches something new.

### Mastery

Player who has completed multiple paths can:
- Explain every faction's perspective sympathetically
- Identify the game-theoretic traps in real situations
- Think through second and third-order consequences
- Appreciate the genuine difficulty of political change
- Approach real political discussions with more nuance

**This is civic education at its deepest.**

## Success Metrics

The game succeeds if players:

1. **Remember characters** - talk about them like real people
2. **Debate endings** - argue about which outcomes were "better"
3. **Change minds** - update their views on Iranian politics
4. **Seek knowledge** - click "Learn More," read the sources
5. **Replay** - want to see other paths, understand other perspectives
6. **Apply learning** - bring insights to real political discussions
7. **Recommend** - share because it's good, not because it's "educational"

---

## A Final Note on Tone

This game is about tragedy - the tragedy of politics, where good people produce bad outcomes, where systems trap individuals, where history constrains the present.

But it is not cynical. It is not nihilistic.

It believes:
- Understanding matters
- Choices matter
- Better futures are possible
- The Iranian people deserve flourishing

It simply refuses to pretend the path is easy or obvious.
