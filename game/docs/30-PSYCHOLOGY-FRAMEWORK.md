# Psychology Framework

## Why People Do What They Do

This document explains how IRAN 14XX models human psychology. Every character - from Khamenei to a street-level Basiji - is driven by comprehensible human motivations.

**Our goal:** Players should finish the game able to explain, in good faith, why even the people they hate acted as they did.

---

## Core Framework: The Five Foundations

Every character is understood through five psychological foundations:

### 1. SURVIVAL - What Do They Fear?

At the most basic level, what threats loom over this person?

**Categories of fear:**
- Physical death or harm
- Imprisonment, torture
- Loss of family members
- Social death (shame, ostracism)
- Economic ruin
- Existential fear (meaninglessness, chaos)

**Example - IRGC General:**
```
SURVIVAL FEARS:
├── Personal: Prosecution, execution if regime falls
├── Family: Children's futures destroyed
├── Institutional: IRGC dissolved, legacy erased
└── Existential: Everything he built was for nothing
```

**Example - Student Activist:**
```
SURVIVAL FEARS:
├── Personal: Arrest, torture, rape in prison
├── Family: Parents pressured, siblings' opportunities closed
├── Social: Friends abandoning her for safety
└── Existential: Dying unknown, unremarkable, having changed nothing
```

### 2. INTERESTS - What Do They Want?

Beyond survival, what are they trying to achieve?

**Categories of interest:**
- Material: Money, property, comfort
- Status: Recognition, respect, honor
- Power: Control, influence, agency
- Belonging: Connection, community, identity
- Meaning: Purpose, significance, legacy
- Justice: Fairness, redress, accountability
- Freedom: Autonomy, choice, expression

**Example - Bazaari Merchant:**
```
INTERESTS:
├── Material (High): Protect wealth, grow business
├── Status (High): Respected in community
├── Power (Medium): Influence but not exposed
├── Belonging (High): Part of bazaar network, family
├── Meaning (Medium): Provide for family, traditional values
├── Justice (Low): System worked for him
└── Freedom (Medium): Wants less interference, not political freedom
```

**Example - Kurdish Teacher:**
```
INTERESTS:
├── Material (Medium): Stable salary, house
├── Status (Medium): Respected in community
├── Power (Low): Not ambitious personally
├── Belonging (High): Kurdish identity, language, culture
├── Meaning (High): Preserving Kurdish heritage for next generation
├── Justice (High): Equal treatment for Kurds
└── Freedom (High): Cultural expression, language rights
```

### 3. BELIEFS - What Do They Think Is True?

What does this person believe about how the world works?

**Categories of belief:**
- Empirical: What is factually true?
- Causal: What causes what?
- Social: Who can be trusted? How do others think?
- Political: How does power work? What's possible?
- Moral: What's right and wrong?
- Metaphysical: Is there God? Afterlife? Divine plan?

**Example - True Believer Sepahi:**
```
BELIEFS:
├── Empirical: Iran is under attack by Western enemies
├── Causal: Without the regime, chaos and invasion follow
├── Social: Protesters are dupes or traitors, not authentic
├── Political: Strong hand is necessary; democracy leads to weakness
├── Moral: Defending Islam justifies harsh measures
└── Metaphysical: God rewards those who defend the faith
```

**Example - Cynical Regime Insider:**
```
BELIEFS:
├── Empirical: The system is corrupt and hypocritical
├── Causal: But all systems are corrupt; this one at least has order
├── Social: Everyone is looking out for themselves, including reformers
├── Political: Gradual change possible; revolution leads to chaos
├── Moral: Pragmatism is the highest virtue in a fallen world
└── Metaphysical: Skeptical but keeps it quiet; rituals are social glue
```

### 4. IDENTITY - Who Do They Think They Are?

What groups, roles, and narratives define this person's sense of self?

**Categories of identity:**
- National: Iranian, Persian, minority identity
- Religious: Muslim, Shia, secular, atheist
- Political: Revolutionary, reformist, royalist, apolitical
- Social: Class, profession, education
- Family: Role in family, lineage
- Generational: Pre-revolution, war generation, post-war, Gen Z

**Example - War Veteran:**
```
IDENTITY:
├── National: Proud Iranian who defended the nation
├── Religious: Traditional Shia, not political Islam
├── Political: Gave blood for this system; betrayal is personal
├── Social: Working class, sacrificed for ungrateful elites
├── Family: Father who sent sons to war, some didn't return
└── Generational: "You young people don't know what we went through"
```

**Example - Diaspora Youth:**
```
IDENTITY:
├── National: Iranian but raised elsewhere; complex relationship
├── Religious: Secular, culturally Muslim at most
├── Political: Dreams of different Iran; guilt for being safe
├── Social: Educated, Western, doesn't fully belong anywhere
├── Family: Parents' trauma shapes everything
└── Generational: Connection to Iran through internet, family stories
```

### 5. CONSTRAINTS - What Limits Their Options?

What structural factors limit what this person can actually do?

**Categories of constraint:**
- Physical: Location, health, access
- Economic: Resources, dependencies
- Social: Relationships, obligations, surveillance
- Institutional: Position, rules, hierarchies
- Psychological: Habits, traumas, cognitive limitations

**Example - Ministry Bureaucrat:**
```
CONSTRAINTS:
├── Physical: Works in ministry building, surveilled
├── Economic: Depends on state salary, pension at risk
├── Social: Colleagues report on each other, family in Iran
├── Institutional: Cannot deviate from official line publicly
└── Psychological: 30 years of learned helplessness
```

## Character Archetypes

### Regime Supporters

#### The True Believer

**Profile:** Actually believes in the Islamic Republic's ideology.

```
SURVIVAL FEARS:
- Regime fall = end of Islamic society
- Personal salvation at stake

INTERESTS:
- Meaning (High): Life has purpose through Islam and revolution
- Belonging (High): Part of something greater
- Justice (High): But justice means Islamic justice

BELIEFS:
- Western liberalism is spiritual emptiness
- The revolution saved Iran from corruption
- Hardship is test; perseverance is rewarded
- Enemies are real and existential

IDENTITY:
- Revolutionary, defender of Islam
- Heir to the martyrs
- Soldier in cosmic struggle

CONSTRAINTS:
- Would lose everything socially if he changed
- Belief system is load-bearing; questioning threatens psychological collapse
```

**Why they matter:** True believers are the hardest to flip but also the most principled. They can't be bought. But they can potentially be persuaded if their core values (justice, meaning) are addressed.

#### The Trapped Functionary

**Profile:** Doesn't believe anymore (if they ever did) but sees no exit.

```
SURVIVAL FEARS:
- Regime might fall and he's on wrong side
- But defection is also dangerous
- Paralyzed between two fears

INTERESTS:
- Survival (High): Get through this alive
- Material (High): Protect what he has
- Family (High): Children's futures

BELIEFS:
- The system is rotten
- But so is everything else
- Change probably leads to chaos
- Better the devil you know

IDENTITY:
- Ambivalent; doesn't identify strongly with regime
- But 30 years have made it who he is
- Shame about what he's been part of

CONSTRAINTS:
- Too implicated to easily defect
- No skills for private sector
- Family depends on his position
- Watched by colleagues
```

**Why they matter:** These are potential defectors. They need: credible amnesty, economic soft landing, a narrative that lets them preserve dignity.

#### The Opportunist

**Profile:** In it purely for personal benefit.

```
SURVIVAL FEARS:
- Losing the position that grants privileges
- Being scapegoated by either side

INTERESTS:
- Material (Very High): Money, property, contracts
- Status (High): Enjoys the perks of power
- Power (High): Ability to make things happen

BELIEFS:
- Ideology is nonsense; everyone's in it for themselves
- The smart play is to be close to power
- If regime falls, be close to new power

IDENTITY:
- Doesn't identify with regime; identifies with success
- Fluid; will adopt whatever identity serves

CONSTRAINTS:
- Wealth is tied to regime connections
- Known as regime figure; can't easily rebrand
```

**Why they matter:** Opportunists will switch sides when the wind changes. They can be bought, leveraged, or threatened. They're useful but unreliable.

#### The Second Generation

**Profile:** Born into regime-connected family; inherited position.

```
SURVIVAL FEARS:
- Father's enemies becoming his enemies
- Being blamed for things he didn't do
- Losing privileges he's always known

INTERESTS:
- Material (High): Accustomed to comfort
- Status (High): Sensitive to perceived slights
- Belonging: Torn between family and peers
- Freedom: Secretly resents constraints of position

BELIEFS:
- Complex; educated, exposed to outside world
- Knows system's flaws but also its logic
- Uncertain what alternative would mean for him

IDENTITY:
- Not a true believer like father
- But can't fully reject family
- "It's complicated"

CONSTRAINTS:
- Name is known; can't be anonymous
- Family expectations and surveillance
- Guilt about privileges vs. peers
```

**Why they matter:** The elite's children are a pressure point. Many are privately skeptical. Some could be bridges to change. Some will fight hardest to preserve what they inherit.

### Regime Opponents

#### The Principled Activist

**Profile:** Motivated by genuine belief in justice and rights.

```
SURVIVAL FEARS:
- Prison, torture, death
- Accomplishing nothing
- Becoming what she fights

INTERESTS:
- Justice (Very High): The wrongs must be righted
- Freedom (Very High): For herself and everyone
- Meaning (High): This is what her life is for

BELIEFS:
- The regime is illegitimate and immoral
- Change is possible through collective action
- Violence should be avoided if possible
- The world is watching and support matters

IDENTITY:
- Activist, defender of rights
- Voice for the voiceless
- Part of long tradition of Iranian struggle

CONSTRAINTS:
- Constantly surveilled
- Limited resources
- Family pressures to stop
- Trauma from past arrests
```

#### The Vengeful

**Profile:** Personally wronged by the regime; wants punishment.

```
SURVIVAL FEARS:
- Dying before seeing justice
- Oppressors escaping accountability

INTERESTS:
- Justice (Overwhelming): Punish those who hurt him/family
- Closure: Need the world to acknowledge wrong

BELIEFS:
- The guilty must pay
- Reconciliation without accountability is betrayal
- Soft measures just enable continuation

IDENTITY:
- Survivor, witness
- Voice of the martyrs
- Cannot forgive on their behalf

CONSTRAINTS:
- Trauma shapes all perception
- Difficulty with pragmatic compromises
- May alienate allies with maximalism
```

#### The Pragmatic Reformer

**Profile:** Wants change but through practical steps.

```
SURVIVAL FEARS:
- Revolution leading to chaos
- Maximalists wrecking chances for change
- Being irrelevant to both sides

INTERESTS:
- Change (High): But achievable change
- Stability (High): Change without destruction
- Influence (High): Wants a seat at the table

BELIEFS:
- Change happens gradually
- Some regime elements can be worked with
- Revolution usually makes things worse
- International support requires moderation

IDENTITY:
- Bridge-builder, pragmatist
- The responsible adult in the room
- Neither extremist nor collaborator

CONSTRAINTS:
- Distrusted by both sides
- "Moderate" easily portrayed as weak
- Dependent on circumstances for leverage
```

### The Masses

#### The Quietly Disaffected

**Profile:** Dislikes regime but just trying to live life.

```
SURVIVAL FEARS:
- Drawing attention
- Children's futures compromised
- Chaos of change

INTERESTS:
- Family (High): Protect, provide
- Stability (High): Predictability
- Dignity (Medium): Not actively humiliated

BELIEFS:
- Politics is dangerous and corrupt
- Nothing he can do will change anything
- Best to keep head down
- Maybe things will change after [leader dies, economy improves, etc.]

IDENTITY:
- Family man, worker
- Not political; just Iranian
- "I'm not one of those people" (either side)

CONSTRAINTS:
- Family responsibilities
- Economic vulnerability
- Risk-aversion
```

**Why they matter:** The silent majority. Their shift determines outcomes. They don't join movements until movements seem likely to win.

#### The Beneficiary

**Profile:** Not ideological but has benefited from system.

```
SURVIVAL FEARS:
- Losing job, connections, status
- Being on wrong side of change

INTERESTS:
- Material (High): What he has
- Security (High): Predictable future

BELIEFS:
- System works for people who work with it
- Protesters are mostly privileged kids
- Change would hurt ordinary people like him

IDENTITY:
- Made it through hard work
- Not elite, not opposition
- Practical man

CONSTRAINTS:
- Job tied to state
- Social circle is pro-regime
- Limited information outside bubble
```

## Psychological Dynamics

### Cognitive Dissonance

When actions conflict with beliefs, people resolve this by:
- Changing beliefs to match actions
- Rationalizing actions
- Avoiding information that creates dissonance

**Example:** The Sepahi who tortured someone initially felt guilt. Over time, he convinced himself: "They were terrorists. I was protecting innocents. I had no choice. Others are worse."

**Game implementation:** Characters who take extreme actions become more extreme over time (commitment escalation). Characters given face-saving options can moderate.

### Sunk Cost

Having invested heavily, people resist admitting it was wrong.

**Example:** The war veteran who lost children can't accept the system they died for was wrong. To accept that is to make their deaths meaningless.

**Game implementation:** Characters with high investment in the regime resist change more, unless given narratives that honor their sacrifices while enabling change.

### Identity-Protective Cognition

People reject information that threatens their identity.

**Example:** The diaspora intellectual who built his identity on "Iran's democratic future" can't process information suggesting democracy might not be best for Iran right now.

**Game implementation:** Characters filter information through identity. Presenting threatening information without addressing identity fails.

### Moral Licensing

Good actions "license" bad ones.

**Example:** The IRGC commander who endows a mosque feels entitled to corruption. "I'm a good Muslim overall."

**Game implementation:** Characters with "good" self-concept may engage in worse behavior, believing their overall balance is positive.

## For Content Authors

When writing any character action or dialogue, ask:
1. **Survival:** What does this character fear?
2. **Interests:** What are they trying to achieve?
3. **Beliefs:** What do they think is true?
4. **Identity:** Who do they think they are?
5. **Constraints:** What limits their options?

If a character acts inconsistently with their psychology, either:
- Revise the action, or
- Explain the inconsistency (stress, new information, character growth)

**The goal is not to make characters sympathetic. It's to make them comprehensible.**

A torturer can be comprehensible without being sympathetic. Understanding why someone became what they are doesn't excuse what they do.

But it makes defeating them possible - because you know what they want, what they fear, and what might change their calculation.
