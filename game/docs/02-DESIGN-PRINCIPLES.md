# Design Principles

These principles guide every design decision in IRAN 14XX. When in doubt, consult these.

---

## Principle 1: Show, Don't Tell

**Wrong:** "Purging officials will destabilize the bureaucracy."

**Right:** You purge officials. Three months later, your reform policies aren't being implemented. Investigation reveals: no one knows how to process the paperwork. The old bureaucrats are gone. The new ones are loyal but incompetent. An advisor says: "We should have kept some of them..."

Let consequences teach. Don't lecture.

---

## Principle 2: Every Choice Has Defenders

For every decision in the game, there must be an intelligent, good-faith argument for each option.

**Test:** Can you write a 200-word defense of this option that would convince a reasonable person? If not, the option is a strawman.

No choice should be "obviously wrong" to a thoughtful player.

---

## Principle 3: Consequences Should Surprise (But Make Sense)

Players should sometimes think: "I didn't see that coming."
But they should never think: "That doesn't make sense."

Every consequence must be:
- Logical in retrospect
- Connected to the choice
- Explicable if the player asks "why?"

**Example:** You ally with the military to ensure stability. Five years later, when you try to expand civilian control, the generals say: "We made you. You owe us." This surprises - but of course it happens.

---

## Principle 4: Model Incentives, Not Personalities

Don't make the IRGC aggressive because "they're bad guys."
Make them aggressive because their institutional interests are threatened.

If their interests were secured, they would behave differently. The game should reflect this.

**This applies to everyone:** Reformists aren't good because they're virtuous - they have incentives toward reform. Change the incentives, change the behavior.

---

## Principle 5: The Map Is Not The Territory (But It's Useful)

Our models are simplifications. Acknowledge this.

- Real Iran has infinite complexity
- We model what matters for the game's purposes
- When players ask "but what about X?" - consider adding it, or acknowledge the abstraction

Useful simplification > false precision.

---

## Principle 6: Respect All Factions

Even factions the designers personally oppose must be:
- Represented by their best arguments
- Given intelligible motivations
- Allowed to succeed under the right conditions
- Treated with the same depth as favored factions

If a monarchist player feels mocked, we've failed.
If a leftist player feels their ideas are strawmanned, we've failed.
If a religious player feels their faith is caricatured, we've failed.

---

## Principle 7: Time Is A Teacher

Consequences should often be delayed.

- Immediate feedback: too gamey, doesn't teach patience
- Long-delayed feedback: teaches that actions echo, builds toward endings

**Structure:** Some consequences are immediate (faction reactions). Some are delayed by months or years (institutional changes, cultural shifts, economic effects).

---

## Principle 8: History Rhymes

When something in the game echoes a real historical event, surface it.

Not as a lecture - as a "Learn More" option:
> "This situation resembles the Spanish transition of 1977-78. Juan Carlos faced a similar dilemma..."

Let players discover patterns. The recognition should feel like insight, not instruction.

---

## Principle 9: Allow Failure Gracefully

Players should be able to:
- Make catastrophic mistakes
- Reach terrible endings
- Destroy Iran (civil war, partition, failed state)

And the game should:
- Let them experience the full consequences
- Not prevent bad outcomes through railroading
- Make bad endings as polished as good ones

**A well-crafted tragic ending is better than a forced happy one.**

---

## Principle 10: Replayability Through Perspective, Not Randomness

The game should be different each time primarily because:
- Different character = different starting position
- Different choices = different outcomes
- Different knowledge (from previous plays) = different strategy

Not because of:
- Random events (minimize these)
- Arbitrary variation

Players should feel: "If I play again as Khamenei, I'll understand more about why he acts as he does."

---

## Principle 11: Complexity Is Earned

Start simple. Add complexity as players demonstrate mastery.

**First playthrough:** Core mechanics, main factions, clear choices.
**Later playthroughs:** More nuanced options, smaller factions matter more, subtle mechanics revealed.

Don't overwhelm new players. Don't bore experienced ones.

---

## Principle 12: Beauty Is Functional

Visual design isn't decoration. It:
- Creates emotional tone
- Aids comprehension
- Rewards attention
- Expresses respect for the subject matter

Every visual choice should have a reason. "It looks cool" is insufficient.

---

## Principle 13: Persian Is Primary

This game is primarily for Persian speakers.

- Persian should be the default/primary language
- English is a secondary audience
- RTL should feel native, not adapted
- Persian cultural aesthetics inform design
- Persian-language scholarship is valued equally with English

If something works in English but feels wrong in Persian, change it.

---

## Principle 14: Mobile Is A First-Class Citizen

Many players will be in Iran on phones with limited bandwidth.

- Design for mobile first, scale up for desktop
- Minimize asset sizes
- Work offline where possible
- Don't assume high-speed connections

A player on a Tehran bus should have a good experience.

---

## Principle 15: AI Augments, Doesn't Replace

AI (LLMs) should:
- Provide depth on demand (explain concepts, draw parallels)
- Enable dynamic conversations with advisors
- Offer analysis of player choices

AI should NOT:
- Generate core narrative (quality control)
- Make up historical facts (accuracy)
- Replace authored character voices (consistency)

Human craft for structure; AI for depth.

---

## Principle 16: Sources Matter

Every claim the game makes about how the world works should be traceable to:
- Academic literature
- Historical evidence
- Expert analysis

The "Learn More" system connects gameplay to scholarship.

This is not just decoration - it's epistemic hygiene. We don't make things up.

---

## Principle 17: Fun Is Non-Negotiable

If it's not engaging, no one will learn.

- Pacing must work (not too slow, not too rushed)
- Stakes must feel real
- Characters must be memorable
- Choices must feel meaningful
- Outcomes must feel earned

Test constantly: Is this fun? Would I play this? Would I recommend it?

---

## Principle 18: Iterate Relentlessly

First drafts are wrong. Test, learn, revise.

- Playtest with diverse players (Iranian/non-Iranian, different political views)
- Listen to confusion (if players don't understand, the game is wrong)
- Kill darlings (if something doesn't work, cut it, no matter how clever)

The shipped game will look different from initial designs. This is healthy.

---

## When Principles Conflict

Sometimes principles conflict:
- "Show don't tell" vs. "Sources matter" (sometimes you need to explain)
- "Respect all factions" vs. "Truth matters" (some things are false even if believed)
- "Complexity is earned" vs. "Model incentives" (full model is complex)

**Resolution hierarchy:**
1. Does it serve player learning?
2. Does it respect human dignity?
3. Is it honest?
4. Is it engaging?

If it serves learning, respects dignity, is honest, and is engaging - the conflict resolves.

---

*These principles should be consulted before making any significant design decision.*
