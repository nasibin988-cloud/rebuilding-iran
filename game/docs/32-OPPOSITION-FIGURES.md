# Opposition Figures: Deep Psychological Studies

This document provides comprehensive psychological profiles of opposition-affiliated characters. The goal is to show that opposition figures are also complex humans with mixed motivations - not simple heroes.

## THE MONARCHIST CAMP

### Reza Pahlavi (Historical Figure, Playable)

**Age at game start:** Mid-60s
**Position:** Crown Prince in exile, claimant to throne

#### The Man Without a Country

**Origin story:**
- Left Iran at age 17, weeks before revolution
- Watched father die in exile, bitter and broken
- Spent 45+ years preparing for a return that never came
- Every year: "Maybe this year..." Every year: disappointment
- Trained as a fighter pilot; never got to serve his country

**The core wound:** He was supposed to be king. His entire identity was built around a destiny that was stolen at 17. Everything since has been waiting.

**What he actually wants:**
```
REZA PAHLAVI'S HIERARCHY OF NEEDS:
1. Return to Iran before he dies
2. Restore family honor (father's legacy rehabilitated)
3. Iran flourishes (genuine; this is his purpose)
4. Some role in new Iran (not necessarily king)
5. His children have a homeland
```

**His genuine beliefs (these are real to him):**
- The Pahlavis modernized Iran; history will vindicate them
- The revolution was a catastrophe, and everyone knows it now
- Constitutional monarchy could unite Iran
- He's above factions; he represents continuity, not ideology
- The Iranian people, freely choosing, would welcome him

**His fears:**
- Dying in exile, having accomplished nothing
- Being used and discarded by foreign powers
- Being seen as a puppet (most painful accusation)
- Iran fragmenting into chaos (feels responsible to prevent)
- His children forgetting they're Iranian

**His constraints:**
- No power base inside Iran
- Dependent on Western attention/support
- Associated with father's SAVAK crimes (unfairly? fairly?)
- Age: diminishing time and energy
- Other opposition factions distrust him

**Strategic implications:**
- His legitimacy claim is powerful but double-edged
- Associating with him gains monarchist support, loses republican support
- He's more pragmatic than his base (would accept reduced role)
- His foreign connections are asset and liability
- He desperately needs a win; this makes him potentially flexible

#### Playable Character Arc

If player chooses Reza Pahlavi:
- Starts outside Iran, with fame but no power
- Must build coalition while managing monarchist hardliners who want full restoration
- Key decision: Insist on crown vs. Accept symbolic role vs. Step aside for democracy
- Player learns: Why the diaspora is so frustrated, why exile politics is so hard

### The Monarchist Hardliner

**Name:** General Farhad Azimi (fictional, composite)
**Age:** 80s
**Position:** Former Imperial Guard, leader of hardline monarchist faction

#### The Last Believer

**Background:**
- Served Shah directly; fled in 1979
- Lost everything: position, wealth, country, friends (many executed)
- Built new life in Los Angeles but never moved on
- Attends every monarchist gathering; gives fiery speeches
- Believes full restoration is the only acceptable outcome

**What he believes:**
- The Shah was right about everything
- The revolution was entirely foreign-orchestrated
- Constitutional limits are what doomed the monarchy
- Iran needs a strong king, not "Western democracy"
- Reza Pahlavi is too soft, too compromising

**What he fears:**
- Dying before seeing the monarchy restored
- Being forgotten; his sacrifice meaning nothing
- Reza making a deal that betrays the Crown
- The truth: that the Shah made mistakes

**His constraints:**
- Old, out of touch with Iran inside
- No actual power; just symbolic loyalty
- His absolutism alienates potential allies
- Lives in the past; 1979 is still yesterday

**Strategic implications:**
- He represents a voting bloc Reza can't ignore
- But his maximalism prevents necessary coalition-building
- He's more useful as symbol than strategist
- His eventual passing will free the monarchist movement to evolve

## THE REFORMIST CAMP

### The Insider Reformist

**Name:** Mohammad Karimi (fictional, inspired by reformist politicians)
**Age:** 60s
**Position:** Former minister, now in a gray zone between tolerated and targeted

#### The Man Who Stayed

**Background:**
- Believed in the revolution; served the system for decades
- Rose through ranks as a pragmatist, reformer
- Supported Khatami's reforms, then Rouhani's
- Watched every reform effort crushed
- Still in Iran; hasn't fled, hasn't been imprisoned (yet)

**The core tension:** Does he stay because he's complicit, or because he's trying to change things from inside? Even he may not know.

**What he wants:**
```
KARIMI'S HIERARCHY:
1. Reform without revolution (preserve gains, prevent chaos)
2. Protect his family (they're in Iran)
3. Remain relevant (can't help if marginalized)
4. Vindication (prove reform was possible)
5. Personal safety (but lower priority than dignity)
```

**His genuine beliefs:**
- The Islamic Republic can be reformed, given time
- Revolution leads to chaos; look at Syria, Libya
- Incremental change is real change
- The hardliners will eventually lose; demography is destiny
- Working within the system is not collaboration

**His fears:**
- Being arrested like his colleagues
- His children being targeted
- Being seen as collaborator by the opposition
- Being proven wrong; reform was never possible
- Chaos that destroys everything he built

**His constraints:**
- Monitored constantly; can't speak freely
- Any move toward opposition risks family
- Can't be seen with foreign governments or exile opposition
- His reformist base has largely given up on system

**Strategic implications:**
- He has networks inside the regime
- He knows where the bodies are buried
- He could be a bridge—or a trap
- His credibility depends on keeping distance from exile opposition
- If regime cracks, he's positioned to play a role

### The Imprisoned Activist

**Name:** Nasrin Mohammadi (fictional, composite of imprisoned activists)
**Age:** 40s
**Position:** In Evin Prison, facing additional charges

#### The Voice They Can't Silence

**Background:**
- Became an activist after personal injustice (arbitrary arrest of family member)
- Started as lawyer helping political prisoners
- Gradually became target herself
- Multiple prison terms; each time came out more determined
- Now famous internationally; regime can't quietly kill her

**What she believes:**
- The Islamic Republic cannot be reformed; it must end
- Violence is not the answer; but neither is patience
- International attention matters; shame is a weapon
- Every day of resistance matters, even if you can't see the impact
- She speaks for those who can't speak

**What she fears:**
- Dying in prison, unknown, unmourned
- Her children forgetting her
- The movement giving up while she's inside
- Being used politically by groups she doesn't trust
- The regime lasting another 40 years

**Her constraints:**
- In prison; can't act directly
- Health deteriorating
- Communication is sporadic
- Can't control how her name is used outside

**Strategic implications:**
- She's a symbol; her release or death matters
- Her smuggled messages can spark action
- The regime holding her is a liability
- Her treatment is a signal of regime intentions

## THE RADICAL OPPOSITION

### The MEK True Believer

**Name:** Maryam (fictional)
**Age:** 40s
**Position:** Mid-level MEK cadre, Ashraf-3

#### The Committed Revolutionary

**Background:**
- Parents were MEK members; born into the organization
- Raised in Iraq camps; knows nothing else
- Genuinely believes in MEK's ideology and leadership
- Has never lived a normal life; the organization is family
- Will do anything for the cause

**What she believes:**
- The MEK are the only true resistance
- Maryam and Massoud Rajavi are divinely guided leaders
- Iran will be liberated through armed struggle
- Other opposition groups are compromised or cowardly
- Personal sacrifice is the highest virtue

**What she fears:**
- Doubt (which occasionally surfaces and terrifies her)
- Abandonment by the organization (only family she knows)
- The leadership being wrong (unthinkable, but...)
- Growing old in exile, nothing changing
- Being on the wrong side of history

**Her constraints:**
- Total institutional control over her life
- Genuine belief mixed with sunk costs
- No skills or relationships outside MEK
- International community sees MEK as cult

**Strategic implications:**
- MEK members can be fierce fighters
- But the organization is too compromised for broad coalition
- Individual members might defect if given genuine alternative
- MEK makes claims about Iran presence that may or may not be real
- Their international lobbying complicates other opposition efforts

### The Armed Resistance Fighter

**Name:** Arash Baluchi (fictional)
**Age:** Late 20s
**Position:** Member of Baluchi resistance group, operating in Sistan-Baluchestan

#### The Man With Nothing Left to Lose

**Background:**
- Family members killed in IRGC operations
- Village destroyed in cross-border operation
- Joined resistance as teenager; only life he knows
- Has killed regime forces; has lost comrades
- No longer imagines a normal life

**What he believes:**
- The regime understands only force
- Peaceful protest is suicide
- The Baluch have been abandoned by everyone
- Independence might be better than being part of Iran
- Better to die fighting than live as a slave

**What he fears:**
- Capture (knows what they'll do to him)
- His family being targeted (again)
- The resistance fading into irrelevance
- Being remembered as a terrorist rather than fighter
- The possibility that it's all futile

**His constraints:**
- Limited weapons, supplies, fighters
- International isolation
- Ethnic isolation from Persian-majority opposition
- Constant survival mode; can't think long-term
- IRGC intelligence penetration

**Strategic implications:**
- Armed resistance ties down regime resources
- But alienates those who oppose violence
- Ethnic dimension complicates national movement
- Could be asset or liability depending on how handled
- His grievances are real; his methods are controversial

## THE DIASPORA

### The Diaspora Intellectual

**Name:** Dr. Dariush Tehrani (fictional)
**Age:** 50s
**Position:** Professor at Western university, public intellectual

#### The Scholar-Activist

**Background:**
- Left Iran for graduate studies; couldn't return
- Built academic career on Iranian politics
- Writes op-eds, gives interviews, builds theory
- Comfortable life in the West; guilt about it
- Advises various opposition factions; trusted by none completely

**What he believes:**
- Understanding is power; ideas matter
- Iran needs liberal democracy, secular government
- The diaspora has a role but shouldn't lead
- International support is essential
- He's providing crucial intellectual infrastructure

**What he fears:**
- Being irrelevant; just another diaspora voice
- Being wrong about what Iran needs (he's been out 30 years)
- His theories failing when tested by reality
- Never seeing a free Iran in his lifetime
- His children not caring about Iran at all

**His constraints:**
- Out of touch with Iran-inside reality
- Seen as elitist by many Iranians
- No operational capacity; just ideas
- Dependent on Western institutions
- Can't verify his information about Iran

**Strategic implications:**
- Provides legitimacy and framing
- Can reach Western policymakers and media
- But his ideas may not match street reality
- His network connects disparate diaspora groups
- His scholarship could inform post-transition planning

## THE LABOR MOVEMENT

### The Oil Worker Leader

**Name:** Hassan Kargar (fictional)
**Age:** Late 40s
**Position:** Informal leader of oil worker organizing

#### The Man Who Can Shut It Down

**Background:**
- Third-generation oil worker
- Seen his standard of living collapse over decades
- Started as non-political; just wanted fair wages
- Organizing led to political consciousness
- Now under constant surveillance; can't be public

**What he believes:**
- Workers have power if they use it
- Oil is Iran's leverage; oil workers are therefore powerful
- The regime fears economic strikes more than protests
- Workers' rights and political freedom are connected
- But: he's not sure what should replace the regime

**What he fears:**
- Being arrested (has family to support)
- Strike failing and being crushed
- Being used by political factions with other agendas
- Workers suffering for gains that never come
- The regime surviving; having risked everything for nothing

**His constraints:**
- Surveillance is constant
- Workers have families, mortgages; hard to ask them to risk everything
- No formal organization (illegal)
- Political opposition groups want to use labor, not serve it
- International sanctions already crushed economy; hard to strike

**Strategic implications:**
- Oil workers striking would be regime-shaking
- But the human cost would be enormous
- He needs credible promises about workers' position post-transition
- His networks could mobilize quickly if conditions right
- He won't be used as cannon fodder for others' political goals

def opposition_effectiveness(coalition, strategy):
    # United front is more powerful
    unity_bonus = 1.0 + (0.1 * len(coalition))

    # But coordination costs rise
    coordination_cost = 0.05 * (len(coalition) ** 2)

    # Inside presence matters more than diaspora
    inside_weight = sum(f.inside_iran_presence * 2 for f in coalition)
    diaspora_weight = sum(f.diaspora_support for f in coalition)

    raw_power = (inside_weight + diaspora_weight) * unity_bonus - coordination_cost

    # Strategy must match capabilities
    strategy_fit = calculate_strategy_fit(coalition, strategy)

    return raw_power * strategy_fit

## KEY OPPOSITION TENSIONS

### The Monarchy Question

**Monarchists believe:**
- Monarchy provides continuity, stability
- Reza Pahlavi has name recognition
- Constitutional monarchy works elsewhere
- It's the only alternative with a "brand"

**Republicans believe:**
- Monarchy is what caused the revolution
- One person family should never rule again
- Democracy means choosing leaders, not inheriting them
- Supporting monarchy alienates most Iranians

**This is not resolvable through argument.** It can only be resolved through:
- Crisis forcing unity (agree to disagree until victory)
- Referendum promise (let the people decide)
- Reza stepping back (removes the issue)
- Republicans accepting symbolic monarchy (trade-off)

### The Violence Question

**Advocates of peaceful resistance:**
- Violence gives regime excuse for crackdown
- International support requires nonviolence
- Armed struggle can't win against IRGC
- Violence poisons the future

**Advocates of armed resistance:**
- The regime only understands force
- 40 years of peaceful protest accomplished nothing
- Self-defense is a right
- Asymmetric warfare can work

**This tension is real.** Different strategies may be appropriate for different circumstances. The game should explore when each might work.

### The Foreign Support Question

**Those who seek Western support:**
- Iranians alone can't defeat the regime
- Sanctions, diplomacy, pressure help
- International legitimacy matters
- Resources and platforms are needed

**Those who reject foreign involvement:**
- Foreign powers have their own interests
- Association with West delegitimizes movement
- Iran's future should be decided by Iranians alone
- Western support comes with strings attached

**Both have valid points.** The game should show how this plays out depending on context.

## CONCLUSION

The opposition is not unified. It contains:
- Maximalists who won't compromise on their vision
- Pragmatists willing to deal
- Those with real inside-Iran capacity
- Those with international platforms but no ground game
- Armed elements and committed pacifists
- Ethnic movements with distinct agendas
- Second-generation diaspora finding their voice

Understanding these distinctions matters because:
- Different opposition actors are useful for different strategies
- Coalition-building requires knowing who can work together
- Post-transition planning requires understanding who wants what
- The regime exploits these divisions; understanding prevents that

**The player who treats the opposition as unified "good guys" will fail** - because they don't understand what they're working with.
